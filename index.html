What are microbotanical remains?

Microbotanical remains are microscopic plant materials preserved within soils, sediments, and archaeological deposits. These remains provide important evidence for reconstructing past vegetation, environmental conditions, and human interactions with landscapes.

Each type of microbotanical remain provides unique insights and preserves under different environmental conditions, and their selection should be tailored to specific project objectives and research questions. When applied together, phytolith and pollen analyses provide complementary evidence, allowing more complete and reliable reconstructions of past environments across a wide range of landscapes.

Phytoliths

Phytoliths are microscopic silica structures that form within plant tissues and cells. Different plant groups produce distinctive phytolith shapes, allowing identification of plant families and functional types under a microscope. Because they are composed of silica, phytoliths are highly durable and often survive long after plants have decayed. In particular, grasses produce abundant and highly diverse phytoliths, making phytolith analysis especially useful in grass dominated ecosystems and archaeological contexts where grasses are of important ecological or cultural significance.

Phytoliths preserve well across a wide range of environments and settings, including both waterlogged settings such as lakes and wetlands as well as dry contexts such as caves, rock shelters, and soils. Their resilience makes them particularly valuable where other organic remains are poorly preserved, providing important insights into past vegetation structure, plant use, and environmental change.

Pollen

Pollen grains are microscopic particles released by plants during reproduction from flowers or cones. Each plant group produces pollen with distinctive shape, size, and surface patterns, allowing identification to family and genus, and sometimes species level, through microscopic analysis.

Plants produce large quantities of pollen that can be transported by wind, water, or animals before becoming incorporated into sediments. Pollen is commonly well preserved in waterlogged environments such as lakes and wetlands but can sometimes also be recovered from soils and archaeological sites including caves and rock shelters. Pollen analysis is particularly effective for reconstructing forest, woodland and heath vegetation, as most trees and shrubs produce unique pollen grains that disperse widely and preserve well in sedimentary records. The identification of pollen and spores provides valuable evidence for reconstructing past plant communities, landscape change, and climatic change.

Charcoal

Charcoal consists of microscopic fragments of burned plant material produced during combustion. These fragments can become incorporated into soils and sediments, where they preserve evidence of past fire activity and vegetation burning.

Charcoal analysis allows reconstruction of fire frequency and vegetation responses to burning through time. Charcoal records can provide important insights into natural fire regime characteristics such as frequency and variability through time. When analysed alongside other microbotanical remains, charcoal helps to build a more complete understanding of past landscape dynamics and human-environment interactions.

What We Offer

Our services are tailored to client needs and we work closely with project partners from sampling design through to reporting to ensure outcomes align with project and regulatory requirements.

Field sampling and strategy

Providing expert guidance on sampling strategies, field collection protocols, and project design to maximise the recovery and integrity of microbotanical evidence.

Laboratory processing

Applying specialised laboratory preparation techniques to safely extract, isolate, and preserve microfossil and sedimentary evidence for high quality analytical outcomes.

Phytolith analysis

Identification and interpretation of silicified plant material from diverse sedimentary contexts to reconstruct past vegetation and cultural activity.

Pollen and spore analysis

Identification and interpretation of fossilised pollen and spores from diverse sedimentary contexts to reconstruct past vegetation dynamics and landscape history.

Diatom analysis

Analysis of silicified algae from sedimentary contexts to reconstruct past water quality and hydrological conditions.

Charcoal analysis

Counting and interpretation of microscopic charcoal to reconstruct fire activity through time.

Reporting

Delivering defensible, client tailored technical reports and project relevant insights prepared to professional reporting and/or publication standards.

Dr. Kelsey Boyd

Co-Director

Kelsey Boyd is an expert in microbotanical and palaeoecological research, specialising in phytolith analysis. Kelsey completed her PhD at the University of Wollongong, Australia, in 2026. Her PhD research focused on developing and evaluating the use of phytolith analysis for reconstructing grassy ecosystems in northern Australia. Her work spans a range of environmental and archaeological projects, providing scientific insights into past landscapes, vegetation dynamics, and human-environment interactions. Kelsey combines rigorous analytical skills with practical applications, supporting heritage management, archaeological research, and environmental assessment.

Dr. Haidee Cadd

Co-Director

hcadd@microbotanicalinsights.com.au

Dr Haidee Cadd is an environmental scientist with advanced expertise in palaeoecology, specialising in palynology and high-resolution environmental reconstruction. Holding both a Master's degree and a PhD focused on palynology, her work integrates microscopy, sedimentary analyses, and multivariate ecological methods to interpret environmental change across diverse landscapes. She has extensive experience analysing pollen from cool temperate rainforests, subtropical systems, and arid environments, enabling robust cross-regional comparisons and nuanced ecological interpretation. Dr Cadd's research centres on multiproxy reconstruction and the application of palynological data to questions of environmental history, site assessment, and landscape evolution. Her technical proficiency and analytical rigour support both academic research and consulting projects requiring precise pollen identification and scientifically robust interpretation.
